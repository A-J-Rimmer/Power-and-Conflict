Ozymandias

Percy Bysshe Shelley


|

I met a traveller from an antique land,
Who said: 'Two vast and trunkless legs of stone
Stand in the desert. Near them, on the sand,
Half sunk a shattered visage lies, whose frown,
And wrinkled lip, and sneer of cold command,
Tell that its sculptor well those passions read
Which yet survive, stamped on these lifeless things,
The hand that mocked them, and the heart that fed;
And on the pedestal, these words appear:
My name is Ozymandias, King of Kings;
Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair!
Nothing beside remains. Round the decay
Of that colossal Wreck, boundless and bare
The lone and level sands stretch far away.'




In the poem, the main subject (i.e. the statue) is ‘second hand’; it’s not the poet or the persona of the poem speaking about what they’ve seen, it’s an unnamed ‘traveller’. This implies insignificance, because the poet himself didn’t feel like it was necessary to go and see the statue for himself. As the statue was supposedly a grand statement of Ozymandias’ power, this undermines the statue, showing the insignificance of human power against the power of nature.

This is an example of sibilance, which emulates the sound of the desert sand moving. The effect continues throughout the poem (“passions” and “lifeless”, for example, have an emphasis on the ‘ess’ sound), making the sound of the desert ever present. This suggests that although the statue has decayed, the desert outlasts it, which supports the theme of the power of nature.

This provides the image of decay, adding to the idea that everything will eventually crumble, regardless of its strength.

This is an example of plosive alliteration, which creates a harsh sound, suggesting Ozymandias was cruel. As a result, the reader feels no sympathy for Ozymandias, which can also be linked to Ozymandias being a metaphor for the current king when Shelley wrote the poem (King George III).

This has an ambiguous meaning; it can be interpreted as “make a replica or imitation of something” or to “Tease or laugh at in a scornful or contemptuous manner” (Oxford Dictionaries). This suggests the sculptor has not just made the statue, but they’ve created it in a way that suggests Ozymandias was not the beloved ruler he thought he was (as suggested by “wrinkled lip” and “sneer” which are both negative suggestions about his character). Consequently, the pride of Ozymandias is undermined, making him seem arrogant.

This line creates juxtaposition, as it starts with the image of power/wealth etc. but ends with the image of ‘nothing’. This contrast between what Ozymandias thought would happen (i.e. that visitors would ‘despair’ because he had so much power) and what actually happened (his empire has disappeared) is an example of situational irony; the statue is meant to be a statement of his greatness, but now it just makes him look weak and insignificant compared to nature and time.

This could be described as an oxymoron, as ‘colossal’ implies greatness; it would have taken a huge amount of time and materials to create a ‘colossal’ statue, yet ‘wreck’ implies decay. The effect of this is to add to the idea that the power of nature has won over the power of humans.

Here, alliteration emphasises the fact that the sand is all that surrounds the statue, with ‘level’ having the connotation of consistency, showing the sand has and will outlast the statue.

This gives the idea of the sand being infinite, but this is impossible, it suggests the sand is a metaphor for time. Ozymandias’ statue has not just been eroded by the sand, but by the passage of time itself.
