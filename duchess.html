My Last Duchess

Robert Browning


That’s my last Duchess painted on the wall,
Looking as if she were alive. I call
That piece a wonder, now: Frà Pandolf’s hands
Worked busily a day, and there she stands.
Will’t please you sit and look at her? I said
‘Frà Pandolf’ by design, for never read
Strangers like you that pictured countenance,
The depth and passion of its earnest glance,
But to myself they turned (since none puts by
The curtain I have drawn for you, but I)
And seemed as they would ask me, if they durst,
How such a glance came there; so, not the first
Are you to turn and ask thus. Sir, ’twas not
Her husband’s presence only, called that spot
Of joy into the Duchess’ cheek: perhaps
Frà Pandolf chanced to say ‘Her mantle laps
Over my lady’s wrist too much,’ or ‘Paint
Must never hope to reproduce the faint
Half-flush that dies along her throat’: such stuff
Was courtesy, she thought, and cause enough
For calling up that spot of joy. She had
A heart – how shall I say? – too soon made glad,
Too easily impressed; she liked whate’er
She looked on, and her looks went everywhere.
Sir, ’twas all one! My favour at her breast,
The dropping of the daylight in the West,
The bough of cherries some officious fool
Broke in the orchard for her, the white mule
She rode with round the terrace – all and each
Would draw from her alike the approving speech,
Or blush, at least. She thanked men, – good! but thanked
Somehow – I know not how – as if she ranked
My gift of a nine-hundred-years-old name
With anybody’s gift. Who’d stoop to blame
This sort of trifling? Even had you skill
In speech – (which I have not) – to make your will
Quite clear to such an one, and say, ‘Just this
Or that in you disgusts me; here you miss,
Or there exceed the mark’ – and if she let
Herself be lessoned so, nor plainly set
Her wits to yours, forsooth, and made excuse,
– E’en then would be some stooping; and I choose
Never to stoop. Oh sir, she smiled, no doubt,
Whene’er I passed her; but who passed without
Much the same smile? This grew; I gave commands;
Then all smiles stopped together. There she stands
As if alive. Will’t please you rise? We’ll meet
The company below, then. I repeat,
The Count your master’s known munificence
Is ample warrant that no just pretence
Of mine for dowry will be disallowed;
Though his fair daughter’s self, as I avowed
At starting, is my object. Nay, we’ll go
Together down, sir. Notice Neptune, though,
Taming a sea-horse, thought a rarity,
Which Claus of Innsbruck cast in bronze for me!




The use of ‘my’ highlights the Duke’s self-importance and possessive nature- he is implying that the duchess belongs to him. This was common in the Victorian era (when the poem was written), where married women were considered the legal property of their husbands.

Although this is posed as a question, the Duke is really demanding that the listener should sit down in order to listen to him speak. This reflects his need to control, as he asks a similar question again, later in the poem (“Will’t please you rise?”), and so emphasises the idea that the Duke feels he is superior to the listener. He remains standing throughout, forcing the listener to look up at him, which provides a visual hint that he is the ‘master’.

This phrase is a metaphor to show the Duke’s need to control the duchess- he is frustrated by her paying attention to others and smiling at them, so he hides her away behind a curtain so that she can only smile for him when he wants her to.

Throughout the poem, the Duke addresses the listener with formal language. Initially, this is to suggest he is polite, but instead it shows how the Duke feels superior to the listener, and wants them to realise the same thing. ‘Thee’ etc. would be far more colloquial, and suggest the two are equals, but instead, the Duke is being condescending and emphasising his superiority.

The Duke repeats the name several times throughout the poem, showing his vanity. In addition, ‘Frà’ means ‘brother’, so Frà Pandolf is likely to be a monk. However, the Duke is accusing him of flirting with the duchess (“Her mantle laps Over my lady’s wrist too much,”), but as a religious figure, this is unlikely to be something that happened. Consequently, it indicates that the Duke is paranoid and unjust.

This is repeated in the poem, suggesting that the fact that the duchess is smiling makes him very angry. This shows that he is being irrational- the Duke in the poem is based on Duke Alfonso II d’Este, whose first wife was only 14 when they married, and who died aged 17 under suspicious circumstances. Therefore, it is likely that the traits she had that enraged the Duke, such as humility and gratitude, are because she was young and innocent. Browning is using satire here, as during the Victorian era, it was widely believed that women were incapable of rational thought, yet here, the Duke is the irrational character.

This phrase is very dismissive, suggesting that the Duke is jealous of everything that receives attention from his wife. He views her as his possession, and so he doesn’t want to ‘share’ her. Once again, it also shows his paranoia- the phrase is hyperbolic, showing he is mistaking her curiosity for flirting.

This indicates that the Duke is obsessed with status- the family of his first wife could be described as ‘nouveau-riche’, and so he feels that his higher social class is something his wife should be very grateful for, even though she would be unlikely to view it as important. His anger at her disregard for this is shown by how he embellishes ‘name’ with adjectives, in an attempt to emphasise the importance it has to him.

This interjection indicates that the Duke is untrustworthy- he is implying that he is not a good speaker, yet his speech is a perfectly structured argument, entirely in iambic pentameter. This shows that the Duke is not who he attempts to portray himself as- his polite tone is condescending, his demands are disguised as questions etc.

This shows the full extent of the Duke’s obsession with status, in that he is more comfortable in ordering his wife’s murder than to momentarily make himself his wife’s equal and actually speak to her.

These lines have an ambiguous meaning- the Duke uses a euphemism to show that he ordered his wife’s murder, yet he hides this from the listener from not admitting outright. This indicates his arrogance- he is admitting to murder, but in a way that makes it clear to the listener that they are inferior and cannot accuse him, because he has not explicitly said that his wife’s death was his fault.

This has an ambiguous purpose- the duke could be indicating that the painting is very realistic, and also that his wife is dead. However, the repetition indicates that he actually prefers his wife as a painting than as a living person, which ties into his need for control.

This suggests that the Duke is not in control of his own speech, as he mentions the dowry he will receive from a second marriage, then seems to backtrack by assuring the listener that his real ‘prize’ will be the companionship of a wife rather than the money. However, this actually gives the opposite impression, thus showing that the Duke is not entirely able to control his personality. This links to the Victorian ideas of duality; the Duke attempts to make himself look like a respectable nobleman, but the reader is also shown another, darker side to him. This would be shocking to audiences at the time this poem was published.

This ends the poem with a metaphor for the Duke- he views himself as the all-powerful Neptune, exercising his power over the Duchess. He feels he is in his domain, and his job is to control women, as was common during the Victorian era, where married women were the ‘possessions’ of their husbands. The poet is suggesting that the Duke feels the need to surround himself with art as symbols of power- he points them out to the listener as decorations, but they are much more to do with ego than an interest in art.
