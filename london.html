London

William Blake


I wander thro' each charter'dstreet,
Near where the charter'd Thames does flow.
And mark in every face I meet
Marks of weakness, marks of woe.

In every cry of every Man,
In every Infants cry of fear,
In every voice: in every ban,
The mind-forg'd manacles I hear

How the Chimney-sweepers cry
Every black'ning Church appalls,
And the hapless Soldiers sigh
Runs in blood down Palace walls

But most thro' midnight streets I hear
How the youthful Harlots curse
Blasts the new-born Infants tear
And blights with plagues the Marriage hearse




This choice of verb connotes a sense of aimlessness, suggesting that the persona in the poem (Blake) and the people around him are repeatedly doing the same mindless activities every day, without doing anything to stop their oppression.

‘Chartered’ shows how humans are trying to use their power over nature, which is ironic (and therefore satirical of human power) as a river can’t really be controlled. However, Blake is suggesting that those in power are not just trying to oppress those they view as ‘inferior’, but nature itself, by turning it into a business.

Between 1760 and 1820, 6 million acres of public land were chartered (made private), which created profit for bankers etc. but put the poor at a disadvantage.

In this case, ‘marks’ has more than one meaning. Initially, it means to notice, but then it shifts to create imagery of damage/suffering (‘marks of woe’). This also introduces the idea that all the people living in the microcosm of London are marred in some way because they are being controlled by those in power.

The different meanings also create a sense of insecurity and reflects Blake’s unhappiness with the way London is changing, where the working class etc. are ignored by the monarchy and the Church etc.

This is an example of anaphora, where the repetition creates a sense of monotony to represent the way the people of London are living their lives. It also enforces the idea of everything being corrupted, and so contributes to Blake’s argument for a revolution.

The use of alliteration emphasises the idea that all the limitations on people are caused by others holding power over them. The imagery of ‘manacles’ would be shocking to an 18th century audience, as they were used for criminals, implying that people were being treated like criminals purely because of their social status.

This use of imagery criticises the industrial revolution, which meant that children as young as 6 were forced to do jobs such as chimney sweeping. The Church was in fact responsible for many of these children, and so the black has a double meaning, as it represents soot, and also evil. Blake is suggesting that the Church, which sees itself as good and holy, is in fact evil because it is ignoring the needs of the poor.

Blake is criticising the monarchy for the oppression, and so he references the French Revolution with a hyperbole created at the time used when addressing the sheer volume of French public executions. He is calling for a similar revolution in Britain.

‘Blasts’ is plosive, indicating violence, which creates juxtaposition between the innocent infant and the violent world they are born into. However, it is also suggesting that life is corrupted from birth.

This oxymoron also serves to suggest that all good things will become corrupted, and could also be a reference to the Church, by implying the Church gives the illusion it is pure and omnibenevolent, but that really it is just as responsible for unhappiness and death as any other person in power.
